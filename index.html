Albert Einstein

Father of Modern Physics

About

Albert Einstein (1879–1955) was a German-born theoretical physicist renowned for developing the theory of relativity, one of the two pillars of modern physics alongside quantum mechanics. His most famous equation, E=mc², revolutionized our understanding of energy and mass. In 1921, he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for his explanation of the photoelectric effect, which helped establish quantum theory. Beyond his scientific achievements, Einstein was a passionate advocate for peace, democracy, and social justice, speaking out against war, racism, and inequality. His work has had a profound impact on various fields, including cosmology, particle physics, and technology, making him one of the most influential scientists in history.

"The World as I See It" (1934): In this essay, Einstein shares his personal reflections on life, philosophy, religion, and the pursuit of knowledge. It's a window into his worldview, emphasizing intellectual humility and the interconnectedness of all people.

Blog 2

"Why Socialism?" (1949): In this essay, Einstein discusses his political views, advocating for a democratic form of socialism. He critiques capitalism and its tendency to concentrate power, arguing for a more equitable distribution of wealth and resources.
